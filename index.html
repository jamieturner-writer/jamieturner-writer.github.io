About Jamie

"When I was six, I thought writing was a way to learn something. Turns out, I was right."

Hi, I'm Jamie Turner. I was raised in a Southern Baptist pew, wandered into atheism in my twenties, and somehow found myself back at the pulpit ‚Äî married to a pastor, no less. It's been a long, complicated walk of faith, full of detours, breakdowns, and unexpected grace.

Raised in the Southern Baptist tradition, I know firsthand how easy it is to confuse religious tradition with true faith. After years of searching, stumbling, and seeking answers, I'm learning ‚Äî and writing ‚Äî about the difference.

I grew up Southern Baptist. Not just on Sundays. I was a quiet kid, but I was always thinking. Always watching. I asked questions in my head long before I ever spoke them out loud. But the church I grew up in didn't really leave space for that. It shaped everything ‚Äî how I talked, how I dressed, how I understood the world and my place in it. Looking back, it felt more like control than community. There wasn't much room for curiosity. There wasn't much room to breathe.

"The hardest thing is meeting people that you don't really know. It's kinda scary, but when you get used to them, it's not scary."
‚Äî Jamie, age 6, Wayne County newspaper

I got married on my eighteenth birthday, thinking I was breaking free. In a way, I was. But that escape turned out to be its own kind of trap. That marriage didn't last long. I was divorced by the next year.

The good part of the story started at the local community college ‚Äî the one everybody goes to. That's where I met my husband. We started talking, and I could tell early on this was different. We got married in 1999, and we've been together for 26 years now. Still here.

What drew us together was the talking. Long, hours-long conversations about everything we'd ever been taught and everything we were trying to figure out. We didn't call it deconstruction then. We just called it staying up late and getting the Bible out. Talking about what it meant, what we believed, what didn't sit right.

When I was six years old, a young woman from our church wrote a little story about me for the local newspaper. I still have the folded black-and-white clipping, worn soft with age. The headline reads, "Writing attracts girl to first grade." I'm grinning toothless in the picture, sitting on our tire swing. My hair was curled under all the way around, a little brown bowl on my head.

"Writing ‚Äî because when you get through writing you can go over it and you might get something out of it."

According to the article, I said I wanted to be a nurse. Well. That didn't turn out. I don't do well with bodily fluids. But what I told her I was most looking forward to was writing.

"When someone does something bad and has to sit in a corner, you get scared and think she's gonna do it to you," she says gravely.

Looking back, that actually makes a lot of sense. I grew up in a Bible-believing home, where we read to learn how to live ‚Äî not just for knowledge, but for behavior, for answers. And I guess I figured out early on that reading and writing were how we got there.

Later, when I became a teacher, I realized not everybody learns that way. I used to get so excited talking about poetry or a good story, but sometimes I'd look up and see a room full of students blinking back at me, unmoved. They were not amused. I shrugged and kept going. I ranted about bad Robert Frost interpretations in pop culture. I mean, if we can't get that right, no wonder we mess up the Bible.

And those messes have turned into entire theologies. Whole clubs. With a lot of members.

Now I write about that too. The faith I was given. The faith I ran from. And the faith I'm returning to, slowly, with open eyes and a tender heart. I write for the ones who are sorting through the damage, for the ones who still love Jesus but have no idea what to do with church people, and for the ones who were told to sit down and be quiet.
